When data centres are fast-tracked, who still gets to speak?

⚠️

AI Growth Zones, "national infrastructure" status and planning reform speed approval — and, by design, narrow the local voice. This is a structural risk, not a proven harm. Here is the trade-off in plain terms.

60-second read · Share freely · PR-004
National not local — who decides on the biggest sites

Routed through the Nationally Significant Infrastructure regime, a data centre is decided by central government, not your council — narrowing local objection and judicial review.

The same mechanisms that remove barriers to investment also remove channels for the community to be heard. The framework currently favours speed.

Benefits are real but their long-term local delivery is unproven. The resource costs are concentrated and certain. Both belong in the decision.

The numbers in plain English
National Decision level for the biggest data centres under NSIP rules — moved away from the local council (Planning Act 2008; Infrastructure Planning Bill)
£5m Per zone for local AI-adoption schemes; 100% of business-rate growth retained locally — genuine support, if it is delivered (UK Government)
566 Data centres in Virginia alone — ~60% of US capacity in three states. Cited as analogy: the burden clusters sharply (US context)
84% Of proposed data centres said to be in soon-to-be water-scarce areas — a campaigner estimate, reported as such, not as established fact
"This is not a paper that says stop building. It says: notice what you are trading away when you fast-track."
Scott Seivwright · The Firewalkers
What we are calling for
→Make the safeguards binding — confirmed water supply and social-value assessment as enforceable conditions, not descriptions.
→Independent oversight of environmental compliance for every fast-tracked zone.
→Community-benefit agreements attached to zone approvals, with defined local channels to be heard.
→Sunset clauses on accelerated powers — speed should not become permanent silence.
