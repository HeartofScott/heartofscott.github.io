⁂ The Firewalkers Press Release Style Guide · v1.0
Internal document · Not for publication

How we write a press release that gets used.

Every press release we publish carries the credibility of the movement. This guide defines the standard — for writing, structure, shareability, and authority. Follow it every time.

Version 1.0 · June 2026 · Owner: Scott Seivwright
01

Philosophy — what a Firewalkers press release is

We are not writing corporate announcements. We are writing documents that give journalists, campaigners, residents, and decision-makers exactly what they need to act. Every release must be readable by an 80-year-old, shareable by a 25-year-old, and citable by a journalist on deadline.

The single most important rule

A press release is not a campaign document. It is a news document. It must contain a fact the world did not know before you published it. If there is no new fact, there is no press release.

Reading age target

Write for a reading age of 12–14 years. Short sentences. Active verbs. No jargon. No acronyms without definition. This is not dumbing down — it is respecting the reader's time. The Guardian writes to this standard. So do we.

Tone

Bold, urgent, honest, grounded. Never hysterical. We make strong claims because we have strong evidence. If we do not have the evidence, we do not make the claim.

Never Hysterical
Avoid Vague alarm
Target Urgent clarity
Avoid Corporate caution
Never Timid
Write like this
  • Britain has applied for three times more data centre electricity than it will need by 2050.
  • A data centre the size of 40 football pitches has been proposed for the Lammermuir Hills.
  • Communities have 12 weeks to respond to planning applications most of them never see.
  • The government admitted it made a legal error. Courts are now deciding the consequences.
  • Scott Seivwright spent 30 years building these systems. He is now fighting to stop them.
Not like this
  • The existential threat of AI infrastructure poses unprecedented challenges to our planet.
  • Stakeholders are increasingly concerned about the hyperscale deployment paradigm.
  • The Firewalkers is committed to raising awareness around this important issue.
  • We believe the time has come for a conversation about responsible technology.
  • Our movement leverages AI capabilities to synergise environmental outcomes.
02

Anatomy — every section, every time

Every press release has the same bones. In this order. Every time. Journalists scan for what they need. Give it to them in the right place.

Top bar

Dark background bar across the top. States: Press Release · [For immediate release OR Embargo until DATE] · [DATE] · [PR-NUMBER]

Always amber text on dark background. Never buried. First thing a journalist sees.
Headline

The single most important sentence in the document. Contains the fact. Written as a news headline — not a slogan, not a question, not a teaser.

Max 12 words. Active voice. Contains a number or a specific claim wherever possible. No full stop.
Standfirst

2–3 sentences that expand the headline into the full story. A journalist who reads only this should understand the news, the source, and the significance.

Left-border in urgent red. 60–80 words. Contains: the finding, the source, and the ask or position.
Meta row

Organisation name, contact name, location, embargo status. Small caps. All on one line.

Always present. Journalists need to know instantly who this is from and how to reach them.
Share bar

LinkedIn, X, WhatsApp, Copy link — immediately below the header, before the body text. Pre-written messages for each platform.

Required on every release. Placed before the lead paragraph so it is seen before reading begins.
Lead paragraph

The full story in one paragraph. Who, what, when, where, why. If someone reads only this, they have the news. Larger font, different weight to body.

Max 60 words. No jargon. Contains the key number or claim. Written for an 80-year-old to understand.
Key facts box

Dark background box with 4 key statistics or facts. Numbers large, labels small. The most citable content in the release.

Required on data-led releases. 4 facts maximum. Each fact sourced — include source in small text below number. No unsourced stats.
Body sections

2–4 sections with small-caps headings. Each section makes one argument. Short paragraphs — 3–4 sentences maximum per paragraph.

Total body length: 400–700 words. If it is longer, cut. Journalists do not read long press releases.
Scott's quote

One pull quote from Scott Seivwright. Should contain an opinion or a position, not a fact. This is the only place opinion belongs. Sounds like a human, not a press officer.

Max 40 words. Must be something a journalist would want to print. If it is forgettable, rewrite it. One quote only — never two.
Author bio

Scott's photo, name, title, 2-sentence description, and credential tags. Standardised across all releases. Links to media page.

Required on every release. Same photo. Same credentials. Same format. Never changes.
Warning / position box

Amber background box. States what The Firewalkers opposes, and crucially what it does NOT oppose. Prevents misrepresentation.

Required. Journalists will quote from this to establish balance. Write it carefully. It is not a disclaimer — it is a position statement.
Notes to editors

Links to full research. Scott's credentials and availability. High-res image availability. Independence statement.

Required. Keep to a bordered box, clearly separated from body. Journalists know to look here for follow-up.
Contact block

Two cards: media enquiries (Scott, email, interview link) and About The Firewalkers (URL, YouTube, LinkedIn).

Required. Media email must be live and monitored. Response time for media: under 4 hours on working days.
Source list

Numbered list of every primary source cited in the release. With title, author, and date where known.

Required on all data-led releases. Never cite secondary sources when a primary is available. No Wikipedia. No press releases from other organisations.
Copyright line

© The Firewalkers [YEAR] · Freely reproducible with attribution · firewalkers.earth

Required. Explicitly invites sharing. Attribution required. See Section 8 for full copyright policy.
— ends —

Centred, small-caps. Traditional press release closing marker. Journalists expect it. Never omit it.

Always present. Always centred. Always the very last line before the footer.
03

The headline — the only sentence that truly matters

80% of people who see a press release read only the headline. Write it for that reader. The headline must contain the news. Not tease it — contain it.

Formula

[Specific number or claim] + [subject] + [consequence or significance]
This forces a real fact into the headline and prevents vagueness.

Strong headlines
  • Britain's data centre queue is more than three times the entire national grid
  • Scotland's data centre water use quadrupled in four years, FOI data shows
  • Hamilton rejects £2bn data centre after 400 residents protest planning meeting
  • UK government admitted planning error — court challenge now under way
  • £64 billion of data centre projects blocked by community opposition in 12 months
Weak headlines
  • New research raises concerns about data centre development
  • The Firewalkers calls for better AI infrastructure planning
  • Community voices matter in the data centre debate
  • Important new findings on AI and the environment
  • The Firewalkers publishes latest research report

The test: Read the headline out loud. Does it sound like something a BBC journalist would say at the start of a story? If yes, it is a headline. If it sounds like a campaign slogan, rewrite it.

04

Writing rules — how to put words together

These rules apply to every word in every release. They are not stylistic preferences. They are the difference between a release that gets used and one that gets deleted.

RuleWhyExample
Short sentences — 20 words maximum Long sentences lose readers. Journalists cut long sentences before they print them. "A data centre can use 1.1 million gallons of water a day. That is equivalent to a town of 10,000 people."
Active voice always Passive voice hides responsibility and weakens claims. "The government approved the application." Not "The application was approved."
Numbers in numerals not words Numbers in numerals are scannable. Numbers in words are not. "50 gigawatts" not "fifty gigawatts"
Define every acronym on first use An 80-year-old resident does not know what NESO, PUE, or GW means. "National Energy System Operator (NESO)"
One idea per paragraph Journalists lift paragraphs whole. Each paragraph must stand alone. 3–4 sentences maximum. White space is not wasted — it is readable.
Source every number Unsourced numbers destroy credibility. One challenge and the whole release falls. Every statistic in body text gets "(Source, Year)" in the source list.
Concrete nouns, not abstractions "The land" is abstract. "The Lammermuir Hills" is real. Real things move people. "ancient moorland" not "green space"; "drinking water" not "water resources"
The position, then the caveat State the strong claim first. Qualify it after. Not the other way around. "The queue is three times the need — on the most conservative measure." Not "On the most conservative measure, the queue may be approximately three times the need."
05

Writing for our personas — who reads this and why

Every release reaches multiple audiences. Write the release for journalists — but design every shareable element for the persona most likely to forward it.

The resident

Wants to know: is this happening near me? What can I do? How much time do I have? Give them a specific location, a deadline, and a direct link to act. Hook them with geography and urgency.

I'm a Resident

Share message template: "A data centre is proposed for [PLACE]. Communities have [X] weeks to object. Here's the research behind the campaign: [URL]"

The technologist

Wants to know: what does the data actually say? Who are the primary sources? Is this rigorous? Give them the numbers, the methodology, and the source list. Hook them with evidence quality and scale.

I'm a Technologist

Share message template: "New data: 50GW queued vs 9.9-14.6GW needed by 2050. The gap is not a rounding error. Full sourced analysis: [URL]"

The activist / ecologist

Wants to know: is this winnable? Has anyone stopped one? What are the grounds? Give them the wins, the legal angles, and the tools. Hook them with evidence that resistance works.

I'm an Activist

Share message template: "£64bn of data centre projects blocked by community action. Here's the evidence, the playbook, and the movement behind it: [URL]"

The journalist / media

Wants to know: is this a story? Is there a quote? Are there pictures? Who can I call? Give them a strong headline fact, a usable quote, and a contact who picks up the phone.

Media

What they need: one sharp quote, one big number, one local angle, one human story, and a phone number that is answered within 4 hours.

07

Shareability — built to travel

A press release that cannot be shared is a press release that will not be read. Every release must have platform-specific share buttons with pre-written messages. No exceptions.

Share bar position

The share bar sits between the meta row and the lead paragraph — before the reader has read a word of body copy. The reader sees it immediately. The impulse to share is highest at the moment of recognition, not after reading 700 words.

This is how the share bar renders:

Share this release X / Twitter LinkedIn WhatsApp
Pre-written share messages — write one per platform for every release
PlatformCharacter limitMessage styleExample
X / Twitter ~200 chars (link takes 23) One sharp claim. Key number. @firewalkersearth tag. 2–3 hashtags. "Britain's data centre queue: 50GW applied for. 14.6GW needed by 2050. New research from @firewalkersearth. #DataCentres #AIImpact"
LinkedIn No limit — 150–200 words optimal Hook → context → significance → link. Professional but urgent tone. "The numbers have just been published and they are stark..." [2–3 paragraphs] + link + 3–5 hashtags
WhatsApp No limit — 2–3 sentences best Conversational. Recommend to a friend. No hashtags. "Worth reading — new research shows Britain has applied for three times more data centre electricity than it will ever need. Full story:"
OG image requirements

Every press release must have an OG image that renders correctly on all platforms. Image must be 1200×630px exactly. No text overlaid. Strong, relevant to the subject. Always use the Unsplash parameter ?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1200&h=630&q=80. Test at opengraph.xyz before publishing.

Copyright — open but attributed

Our press releases are designed to be shared, quoted, and reproduced. We want maximum reach. We require attribution. Every release carries this explicit permission.

© The Firewalkers [YEAR]. This press release may be freely reproduced, quoted, and distributed in whole or in part, in any medium, provided that attribution is given to The Firewalkers and a link to firewalkers.earth is included. No permission required. No notification required.

The Firewalkers accepts no advertising and has no commercial relationship with any data centre operator, energy company, or planning authority. Our research is independent.

Why this matters: A standard "all rights reserved" copyright on a press release actively prevents journalists and campaigners from sharing it. Our explicit open licence is a competitive advantage. It removes every barrier to reproduction.

Pre-publication checklist

Run through every item before a release goes live. No exceptions. One item missed can undermine the credibility of the whole document.

Content
  • Headline contains a specific fact or number — not a slogan
  • Lead paragraph answers: who, what, when, where, why in under 60 words
  • Every statistic in the body is sourced in the source list
  • Scott's quote sounds human — not like a press officer wrote it
  • Author bio is present with photo, credentials, and contact
  • Position box states what we oppose AND what we do not oppose
  • Notes to editors includes availability for interview
  • Ends with "— ends —"
  • Reading age test passed — read aloud, any sentence over 25 words is rewritten
  • Copyright line present — "freely reproducible with attribution"
SEO & Meta
  • Title tag: max 60 chars, keyword-first, matches headline
  • Meta description: 140–160 chars, contains primary keyword, compelling hook
  • Canonical URL set correctly to this page's URL
  • og:title, og:description, og:image all present and correct
  • og:image is exactly 1200×630px with correct Unsplash parameters
  • og:image:width="1200" and og:image:height="630" set explicitly
  • og:locale="en_GB" present
  • article:published_time set to today's date
  • article:author="Scott Seivwright"
  • twitter:site="@firewalkersearth" present
  • twitter:image:alt describes the image
  • Schema.org NewsArticle structured data complete
  • OG preview tested at opengraph.xyz — image loads, text is correct
  • Page added to sitemap.xml
Shareability
  • Share bar present — LinkedIn, X, WhatsApp, Copy link
  • X share link includes pre-written tweet text with @firewalkersearth and hashtags
  • WhatsApp share link includes pre-written conversational message
  • LinkedIn share link present (URL only — LinkedIn generates its own preview)
  • Copy link button tested — copies correct URL, changes to "✓ Copied"
  • OG preview checked on opengraph.xyz
Distribution
  • Media email sent to press list
  • LinkedIn post scheduled — hook + context + link + hashtags
  • X post scheduled — sharp claim + URL + hashtags
  • WhatsApp community post sent
  • Instagram story posted with link in bio
  • Plausible goals checked — verify tracking is working

Reference press releases — learn from the best

These are exemplary press releases from organisations doing similar work — strong evidence, urgent tone, clear structure, wide reach. Study them before writing a new release.

The Firewalkers · PR-001 Britain's data centre queue is more than three times the size of the entire national grid — our own benchmark release. Study the structure, key facts box, and quote.
Global Action Plan Hyperscale data centre protests — The Ecologist coverage. Note how the campaign message translates into a news story. This is what good placement looks like.
Foxglove / Computer Weekly UK government data centre planning decisions queried — note the precision of the claim ("serious logical error") and how it drove coverage. One precise legal fact = a story.
ERCS Scotland Data centres would double Scotland's energy demands — strong local number, clear sourcing, immediate policy relevance. The headline does all the work.
Data Center Watch $64 billion of data centre projects blocked — the definitive tracker. Note how the research report structure feeds directly into quotable press release content.
United Nations University Environmental cost of AI's energy use — UN-standard release. Strong headline numbers, clean structure, clear position. Note the land footprint figure (14,500 sq km) — a striking anchor stat.
Washington Times Erin Brockovich's data centre map tops 5,000 reports — note how a famous name + a precise number = a news story that spreads. The credibility transfer from a known figure is significant.
Surfers Against Sewage SAS press releases — not data-centre related but a masterclass in environmental movement communications. Short, urgent, human, quotable. Their release structure is worth studying in full.
