We do not flee from technology. We walk through it with our eyes open.

Extraction burns what took centuries to grow. The land has taught us this — in clear-cut forests, in mined hillsides, in rivers that no longer run. Now it teaches us again, in server farms and speculation and data harvested like there is no tomorrow to tend.

The Pattern We've Seen Before

We have seen this fire before.

In 1845, the British Parliament approved 272 new railway acts in a single year. A thousand companies formed. Fortunes staked on speculation. Landscapes torn open. Communities uprooted. Most companies failed within a decade — but the tracks remained. And so did the damage.

"Unfettered capital extracts and destroys, then moves on. The land does not move on."

The same fire is burning again. AI as the new railway. Nvidia's market cap briefly exceeded three trillion dollars — not because the technology had matured, but because speculation demanded it. Data centres now consume more electricity than many countries. The water to cool a single large server farm rivals a small city's entire consumption.

The chips are the railway shares. The data centres are track laid through the commons. And when this wave consolidates — as they all do — the extraction remains. The cost stays in the land, not the ledger.

The druidic tradition has a word for this: taking without tending. It has never ended well.

What the Land Teaches

The path of the firewalker

These are not new ideas. They are very old ideas applied to a new crisis. The druid's path was always about right relationship — with land, with community, with what is taken and what is given back.

I

Stewardship over extraction

You do not strip the grove and leave. You tend what you take from. Every server, every chip, every dataset has a cost to the living world. That cost must be acknowledged, minimised, and where possible returned. Sustainable tech is not a brand value. It is a duty.

II

The commons belong to all

The internet was born as commons — a shared path, freely walked. Enclosure is not progress. Open standards, open source, and the right to repair are not idealism. They are the difference between a path through the forest and a tollgate at its edge.

III

Cycles and regeneration

Technology must work within the limits of natural cycles — energy, materials, attention, time. What cannot be regenerated must not be consumed as if infinite. Rare earths are rare. Water is precious. Code that wastes both is not clever. It is careless.

IV

The bard's duty: speak

In the druidic tradition, the bard's purpose was to name what others would not. To hold power accountable through story, the spoken word, the poem that lands like a stone. That duty does not end because the audience is now a board of directors.

How to Walk

Use your voice

The fire only spreads if we carry it. Three paths through the technology thicket — pick the one that fits your hands.

✍️

Write to those with power

Leaders respond to constituents. A well-aimed letter — to an MP, a board, a CEO — matters more than a thousand silent scrolls past a news story.

  • Write to your MP on data centre energy
  • Push your board on sustainable procurement
  • Demand right-to-repair legislation
  • Question speculative AI spend in your org

📖

Share knowledge

The druid's path runs on shared knowledge. What you know, someone else needs. Write it down and bring it to the hearth.

  • Essays, analyses and long reads
  • Research and data sources
  • Case studies of sustainable tech
  • Practical guides for organisations

Make better choices

Individual choices aggregate into markets. Markets shape what gets built. This is slow work — but it is the lasting kind. Roots hold.

  • Choose open-source alternatives
  • Measure your org's tech carbon
  • Question each "AI solution" for real need
  • Support right-to-repair in purchases
