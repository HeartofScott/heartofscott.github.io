The Moment We Are In

We have seen this pattern before.

In the 1840s, railway mania transformed Britain in a decade. Genuine innovation. Real transformation. And vast speculative excess that extracted, consolidated and moved on — leaving the damage behind.

The communities who lived along the routes were rarely consulted. The land was surveyed, acquired and built upon before anyone thought to ask.

We are watching the same pattern now. Dressed in silicon. Moving faster. With far greater consequences for the living systems that sustain everything.

The AI revolution is the fastest land grab in human history. And most of it is happening in 12-week planning windows that communities don't know exist.

We are not anti-AI. We are not against progress. We are against the idea that the earth is a blank space on a planning map — and that the future belongs only to those who move fastest.

Technology should leave the world richer than it found it. That is not a constraint on innovation. It is the condition under which innovation becomes worth having.
