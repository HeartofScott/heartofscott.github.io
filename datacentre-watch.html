§ 01 — UK Intelligence Briefing8 items

The land grab happening
right now.

Planning applications for data centres are moving faster than public scrutiny can keep pace. Here is what is happening, where, and what communities can do about it.

Lead Story — England

England’s Data Centre Applications Doubled in a Single Year — and Approvals Are Keeping Pace

The volume of planning applications for large-scale data centre facilities in England roughly doubled between 2024 and 2025, straining local planning departments unequipped to assess the technical, environmental and infrastructure implications.

The surge reflects the AI infrastructure investment wave, with hyperscalers and colocation operators racing to secure land and grid connections. Planning officers in multiple local authorities have flagged they lack specialist expertise to properly assess applications — particularly around grid impact, water consumption modelling, and cumulative landscape effects.

The doubling in applications has not been accompanied by any equivalent increase in public scrutiny frameworks, EIA thresholds, or community consultation requirements at national level. Communities typically have a 12-week window to respond to applications that will shape their landscape for decades.

This is the same pattern The Firewalkers identified in the railway mania analysis: genuine infrastructure need, but at a speed and scale that deliberately outpaces democratic oversight.

Policy — National

AI Growth Zones: Green Belt Bypass Written Into Policy

The UK government’s AI Growth Zones framework explicitly enables data centre development in locations otherwise protected under Green Belt policy. Blackstone’s 350-acre Northumberland site — on agricultural land — is among the early test cases. Local authorities have limited grounds to refuse qualifying applications.

MHCLG / AI Growth Zones Policy
Planning Precedent

The Didcot Principle: Brownfield as the Non-Negotiable Benchmark

The approved Didcot brownfield data centre is the model every greenfield application must now be tested against. The sequential test — requiring developers to demonstrate no suitable brownfield land exists — is a formal planning mechanism that has delayed and redirected applications.

Russell-Cooke, Planning Analysis
NPPF — Policy Change

NPPF 2024: What Changed and What Counter-Arguments Remain

The 2024 revision to the National Planning Policy Framework introduced changes that directly affect how data centre applications are assessed in England, including revised weight given to strategic infrastructure benefits. PWA Planning identifies the paragraphs operators are relying on — and the objection grounds that remain.

PWA Planning, NPPF 2024
Grid & Infrastructure

Grid Connection Queues Are Reshaping Where Development Goes

National Grid’s connection queue — stretching over a decade for some South East substations — is redirecting data centre investment toward Scotland, the North East, and Wales. Communities there may not have anticipated being targeted. Grid availability, not community suitability, is becoming the primary site-selection criterion.

National Grid / Ofgem, 2025
Landscape — Scotland

Special Landscape Areas: Designation Versus Reality

Scotland’s Special Landscape Area designations are facing their first major stress test in the data centre era. The Lammermuirs campaign is challenging whether the designation constitutes a hard planning barrier or merely a material consideration that can be outweighed by economic benefit arguments. The legal question remains live.

See Lammermuirs campaign ↓
Section 106

Community Benefit Obligations: Winning Concessions Where You Can’t Win Refusal

Where development is likely to proceed, Section 106 agreements can bind developers to specific, enforceable commitments: local employment targets, community energy revenue-sharing, habitat restoration, and limits on future expansion. Quality of obligations depends on how well communities engage before consent is granted.

What Is Proposed

Industrial-scale facility on protected upland moorland

§ 03 — Water & Energy

The hidden costs
every application obscures

Energy and water figures are routinely buried in technical appendices or omitted entirely. Here is what communities should know — and demand to see.

Water

26 Million Litres Per Year — For a Small Site

Analysis from Russell-Cooke and Savills finds that even a modest sub-hyperscale data centre can consume over 26 million litres of water annually for cooling — 520 times a typical UK household’s annual use.

In water-stressed catchments or ecologically sensitive upland areas, this is a material planning consideration. Communities have successfully demanded standalone Water Impact Assessments as a condition of consent. Engage the Environment Agency or SEPA as statutory consultees.

Russell-Cooke / Savills Analysis
Energy

Ireland’s Warning: 22% of National Electricity and Rising

Ireland’s data centres already consume approximately 22% of national electricity — up from near-zero a decade ago. The Irish grid regulator has warned the trajectory is unsustainable. Scotland and parts of England are on a similar path.

AI training workloads are dramatically more energy-intensive than previous computing generations. Planning applications routinely understate operational energy demand by basing figures on projected capacity utilisation rather than actual workload profiles. Demand a verified, independently audited energy assessment.

Irish Times / An Taisce, 2025
What to Demand

A Water Impact Assessment as a planning condition

Formally request a standalone Water Impact Assessment covering: annual consumption volumes; source of water supply and effect on local catchment; discharge quality; impact on watercourses and groundwater; and mitigation measures with binding monitoring obligations.

What to Demand

A verified, audited energy and carbon assessment

Require independently audited figures for: annual energy consumption at full operational load (not design capacity); source and verifiability of renewable energy claims; carbon emissions across full lifecycle; grid connection impact; and a committed, enforceable decarbonisation timeline.

What to Demand

Transparent reporting as an ongoing planning condition

Push for ongoing, publicly accessible annual reporting on energy, water, and environmental performance as a condition of consent — not just at application stage. Many planning conditions are agreed and never enforced. The mechanism exists; communities must insist it is applied.

§ 04 — International Movements

The same fight
everywhere that matters

Communities across three continents are asking the same questions. Strategies and legal arguments that work in one place can be adapted for another.

United States

230+ Organisations Call for a US Moratorium

A coalition of over 230 US environmental, food sovereignty and community organisations — coordinated by Food & Water Watch and reported by TechCrunch — have formally called for a moratorium on new data centre construction pending comprehensive assessment of energy, water and community impacts.

The signatories include environmental justice groups arguing that hyperscale facilities are systematically sited in lower-income communities with less political capital to resist them. The coalition’s evidence base is freely available for UK campaigns to cite.

TechCrunch / Food & Water Watch
Ireland

“Not Here, Not Anywhere”: Ireland’s National Network

Ireland has a coordinated national resistance movement connecting local groups into a network that shares legal strategies, planning objection templates, and political advocacy.

The “Not Here, Not Anywhere” framing — which does not oppose data infrastructure in principle, but insists on right siting and community consent — maps directly onto The Firewalkers’ “pro-wisdom, not anti-AI” position and has proved effective against the “anti-progress” counter-argument.

Irish Times / An Taisce, 2025
§ 05 — Community Resistance Toolkit6 tactics

The tools communities
already have

Most communities fighting data centre proposals don’t know the power already available to them inside the planning system. These six mechanisms are legally established, free to use, and have worked elsewhere.

01

Brownfield Sequential Test

Formally challenge any greenfield or protected-land application by requiring the developer to demonstrate they have assessed and rejected all suitable brownfield alternatives. Use the Didcot approval as evidence that compliant brownfield delivery is achievable — not merely theoretical.

Planning Policy
02

EIA Demand

Request a full Environmental Impact Assessment covering grid connection impact, water use, noise, landscape character, biodiversity net gain, and cumulative effects. Applications that fall below EIA thresholds can still be challenged if they form part of a wider pattern of development by the same operator.

Environmental Law
03

Water Impact Assessment

Demand a standalone Water Impact Assessment as a pre-condition of any consent. Cite the 26 million litres per year figure for small sites. Engage the Environment Agency or SEPA as statutory consultees — they have standing to require assessment on hydrological grounds.

Water Law
04

Section 106 Community Benefits

Use Section 106 agreements to negotiate binding commitments: local employment, community energy revenue-sharing, habitat restoration, and limits on future expansion. Obligations must be specific and enforceable — not aspirational targets that evaporate after consent.

Planning Obligations
05

Landscape Designation Challenge

Challenge any assertion that an AI Growth Zones designation displaces statutory landscape protection for Green Belt, AONB, National Scenic Area, or Special Landscape Area sites. This is a live, unresolved legal question. Secure specialist planning counsel early.

Landscape Law
06

Allied Movements Network

Connect with Save the Lammermuirs in Scotland, Ireland’s Not Here Not Anywhere network, and the US Food & Water Watch coalition. Share legal strategies, planning objection templates, and media contacts. An isolated local campaign is far more vulnerable than a networked one.

§ 06 — AI Prompts for Data Centre Campaigns

Use AI to fight
what AI is building

These prompts are adapted specifically for data centre planning campaigns. Paste them into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — any AI assistant. Replace the brackets with your specific details.

📄

Analyse a data centre planning application

I am reviewing a planning application for a data centre in [location]. Here is the key content: [paste text]. Please summarise: 1) The main proposed impacts on land, water, ecology and community. 2) What information appears missing or understated, especially water consumption, grid connection impact, and noise. 3) The strongest grounds a local community group could use to object. 4) Which statutory consultees should be formally notified.
✉️

Draft a formal planning objection letter

Write a formal planning objection letter from a local resident about a proposed data centre at [location]. My specific concerns are: [list your concerns, e.g. water use, landscape, Green Belt, brownfield sequential test]. Please include: a clear statement of objection; the specific planning policy grounds; reference to NPPF 2024 and relevant Scottish or Welsh planning policy; and a request for independent EIA. Keep the tone professional and evidence-based.
🌊

Challenge a water or energy assessment

I have a water impact assessment and energy report for a proposed data centre in [location or catchment area]. Here is the relevant section: [paste text]. Please explain: 1) Whether the water consumption figures are based on full operational load or design capacity. 2) What has been assessed and what appears to have been omitted. 3) Whether the mitigation measures proposed are adequate or minimal. 4) What questions a community group should formally put to the Environment Agency or SEPA as statutory consultees.
⚖️

Apply the brownfield sequential test

I am challenging a data centre planning application in [location] on brownfield sequential test grounds. The proposed site is [describe: greenfield, agricultural, SLA, Green Belt]. Please: 1) Summarise what the brownfield sequential test requires developers to demonstrate under current planning policy. 2) Identify what evidence the developer should have submitted. 3) Draft formal representations to the LPA requesting confirmation of sequential test compliance. 4) Reference the Didcot brownfield data centre approval as precedent for viable brownfield delivery.
